Interrupted TS Analysis

Crude oil prices are highly sensitive to geopolitical events that disrupt global energy supply and alter market expectations. As a key benchmark for U.S. crude oil, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) reflects real-time assessments of global oil market conditions. Large-scale geopolitical conflicts, especially those involving major energy producers, can therefore generate abrupt and persistent changes in oil price dynamics.

On February 24, 2022, Russia launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine, marking a major escalation in geopolitical tensions and triggering widespread concerns over energy supply disruptions. Russia is one of the world’s largest oil exporters, and the outbreak of the war led to immediate uncertainty regarding production, transportation, and sanctions affecting global oil markets. This event represents a clearly defined and exogenous shock to oil prices.

This analysis employs an Interrupted Time Series (ITS) framework to examine whether the outbreak of the Russia–Ukraine war resulted in a structural change in WTI oil prices. Specifically, the model tests for an immediate change in the price level following the invasion, as well as a change in the post-intervention price trend. By comparing price dynamics before and after February 24, 2022, the ITS approach provides a transparent and intuitive method for assessing how geopolitical shocks are incorporated into oil market prices.

WTI exhibited a clear level shift and short-term high volatility near the intervention point, with prices quickly surging above $100 before retreating but remaining within a relatively high volatility range.

# Fit the interrupted time series regression model
model <- lm(Y ~ Xt + Zt + Pt, data = dataTS)

# Add fitted values to the dataset
dataTS$Y_hat <- predict(model)

# Create interactive plot

              name = "Observed",

Predicted outcomes and their Counterfactuals

Fitting the model:

Code 
# Load required libraries
library(broom)

# Fit the interrupted time series model
regTS <- lm(Y ~ Xt + Zt + Pt, data = dataTS)

# Tidy the model output
summary_table <- tidy(regTS)

# Add significance stars manually
summary_table <- summary_table %>%
  mutate(
    stars = case_when(
      p.value < 0.001 ~ "***",
      p.value < 0.01 ~ "**",
      p.value < 0.05 ~ "*",
      p.value < 0.1 ~ ".",
      TRUE ~ ""
    ),
    term = recode(term,
                  "Xt" = "Time",
                  "Zt" = "Russia-Ukraine Conflict Indicator",
                  "Pt" = "Time Since Russia-Ukraine Conflict",
                  "(Intercept)" = "Constant"),
    `Estimate (SE)` = paste0(round(estimate, 2), stars, "\n(", round(std.error, 2), ")")
  )

# Select and print the desired columns
summary_table %>%
  select(term, `Estimate (SE)`) %>%
  kable(col.names = c(" ", "Estimate (SE)"), align = "l", escape = FALSE) %>%
  kable_styling(full_width = FALSE)

Let’s interpret our coefficients:

  • The Time coefficient indicates the trend in oil prices before the intervention (Russia-Ukraine Conflict). It’s positive and highly significant, showing that prices were steadily increasing over time. Specifically, for each quarter that passed before Russia-Ukraine Conflict, oil prices increased by 0.17 units.

  • The Conflict Indicator coefficient represents the immediate effect of the conflict. While the estimated effect is positive (15.74), it is not statistically significant, suggesting that there was no clear or consistent immediate change in WTI oil prices right at the onset of Russia-Ukraine Conflict.

  • The Time Since Russia-Ukraine Conflict coefficient captures how the trend changed after the intervention. It is negative and highly significant, indicating that after Russia-Ukraine Conflict coefficient began, the rate of decrease in wages and salaries accelerated. For each quarter following the start of the conflict, oil prices decreased by an additional 0.97 units.

  • The Constant term (15.24) represents the estimated level of WTI oil prices at the beginning of the time series (i.e., at time zero).

Delayed Effects of the Russia-Ukraine Conflict Intervention

Code 
# Predict from your actual model
pred1 <- as.numeric(predict(regTS, newdata = dataTS))

datanew <- data.frame(Xt = 1:nrow(dataTS), Zt = 0, Pt = 0)
pred2 <- as.numeric(predict(regTS, newdata = datanew))

T0 <- 386   # shock starts
T1 <- 425   # delayed effect (optional)


y_min <- min(dataTS$Y, na.rm=TRUE)
y_max <- max(dataTS$Y, na.rm=TRUE)
yr <- y_max - y_min
pad <- 0.12 * yr

par(mar = c(5, 5, 4, 8), xpd = NA)

col_pts <- adjustcolor("gray55", alpha.f = 0.35)
col_pred <- "dodgerblue4"
col_cf   <- "darkorange2"
col_gap  <- adjustcolor(col_cf, alpha.f = 0.12)

plot(dataTS$Y,
     col = col_pts, pch = 16, cex = 0.7,
     xlim = c(1, nrow(dataTS)),
     ylim = c(y_min - pad, y_max + pad),
     xlab = "Time (quarters)", ylab = "Prices",
     main = "Interrupted Time Series: Impact of Russia–Ukraine Conflict",
     bty = "l")


rect(T0, par("usr")[3], nrow(dataTS), par("usr")[4],
     col = adjustcolor("gray95", alpha.f = 0.8), border = NA)


idx <- T0:nrow(dataTS)
x <- idx
y_lower <- pmin(pred1[idx], pred2[idx])
y_upper <- pmax(pred1[idx], pred2[idx])
polygon(c(x, rev(x)), c(y_lower, rev(y_upper)),
        col = col_gap, border = NA)


lines(1:nrow(dataTS), pred1, col = col_pred, lwd = 3)
lines(T0:nrow(dataTS), pred2[T0:nrow(dataTS)], col = col_cf, lwd = 3, lty = 2)


abline(v = T0, col = "red3", lty = 2, lwd = 2)
abline(v = T1, col = "forestgreen", lty = 2, lwd = 2)


text(T0 + 2, y_max + 0.03*yr, "Start of Conflict", col = "red3", pos = 4)
text(T1 + 2, y_max - 0.05*yr, "Delayed effect", col = "forestgreen", pos = 4)

legend("bottomright", inset = 0.02,
       legend = c("Observed", "Model prediction (with shock)", "Counterfactual (no shock)", "Estimated impact (gap)"),
       col    = c("gray55", col_pred, col_cf, col_gap),
       pch    = c(16, NA, NA, 15),
       lty    = c(NA, 1, 2, NA),
       lwd    = c(NA, 3, 3, NA),
       pt.cex = c(0.7, NA, NA, 1.5),
       bty = "n")

The Russia-Ukraine conflict had a significant but not immediate impact on WTI crude oil prices. At the onset of the conflict (T₀), oil prices experienced a marked short-term jump and increased volatility, reflecting the market’s rapid pricing of geopolitical risks. However, this shock did not immediately translate into a stable new trend.

After the delayed effect point (T₁), a persistent deviation between the model’s predicted path and the counterfactual scenario indicates that the conflict’s impact on oil prices is primarily reflected in changes to the medium-term trend structure, rather than a one-off shock. This is consistent with the time required for energy supply adjustments, sanctions transmission, and global energy reallocation.

Overall, this time-series analysis suggests that the impact of the Russia-Ukraine conflict on oil prices is more like a “delayed structural shock” than a simple short-term price jump.
