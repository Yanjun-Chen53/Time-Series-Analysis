Global Renewable Energy Growth Trends

The data, sourced from the International Energy Agency (IEA), highlights a clear structural shift in the global energy system. While fossil fuels and hydro power historically dominated capacity additions, the past two decades have been characterized by the rapid acceleration of solar and wind energy. In particular, solar capacity has exhibited exponential growth since the early 2010s, outpacing all other sources, while wind energy has demonstrated steady, sustained expansion. By contrast, hydro capacity additions have remained relatively stable, reflecting geographical and environmental constraints. Nuclear additions appear limited in scale, indicating stagnation compared to renewables. Overall, the figure underscores the transition towards low-carbon energy systems, with solar and wind emerging as the primary drivers of global renewable capacity growth.

Oil Prices vs. Renewable and Traditional Energy ETFs

This figure presents a comparative time-series analysis of West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices (sourced from the Federal Reserve Economic Data, FRED) alongside two exchange-traded funds (ETFs) from Yahoo Finance: the iShares Global Clean Energy ETF (ICLN), representing the renewable energy sector, and the Energy Select Sector SPDR Fund (XLE), representing traditional fossil-fuel–based energy firms.

The visualization employs a dual-axis line chart, with WTI oil prices displayed on the left axis and the ETF prices displayed on the right axis. This design enables a clear comparison of the dynamics between commodity markets and equity markets tied to energy. The temporal alignment of the series allows for the identification of co-movements and divergences.

From the figure, one can observe several key patterns. Periods of sharp volatility in oil prices—such as the downturn around 2008 and the collapse in early 2020—are mirrored by fluctuations in energy-sector equities, though the magnitude and persistence of these responses differ across renewable and traditional ETFs. Notably, while XLE tends to correlate more closely with oil price movements, ICLN demonstrates partial decoupling, reflecting the structural shift toward renewable energy sources whose valuation drivers are less directly dependent on crude oil prices.

Code 
etf_symbols <- c("ICLN", "XLE")

etf_data <- tq_get(etf_symbols,
                   from = "2005-01-01",
                   to = Sys.Date(),
                   get = "stock.prices")

etf_data <- etf_data %>%
  select(symbol, date, adjusted)

wti <- read.csv("data/WTIprice.csv")

wti <- wti %>%
  rename(date = observation_date) %>%
  mutate(date = as.Date(date)) %>%
  rename(WTI = POILWTIUSDM) %>%
  filter(!is.na(WTI))

fig <- plot_ly() %>%
  
  add_lines(data = wti, x = ~date, y = ~WTI,
            name = "WTI Crude Oil", yaxis = "y1",
            line = list(color = "black")) %>%
  
  add_lines(data = subset(etf_data, symbol == "ICLN"),
            x = ~date, y = ~adjusted,
            name = "ICLN (Renewable ETF)", yaxis = "y2",
            line = list(color = "green")) %>%
  
  add_lines(data = subset(etf_data, symbol == "XLE"),
            x = ~date, y = ~adjusted,
            name = "XLE (Energy ETF)", yaxis = "y2",
            line = list(color = "blue")) %>%
  layout(
    title = "WTI Oil Price vs Renewable (ICLN) and Energy (XLE) ETFs",
    xaxis = list(title = "Date"),
    yaxis = list(title = "WTI Oil Price (USD)", side = "left"),
    yaxis2 = list(title = "ETF Price (USD)", overlaying = "y", side = "right"),
    legend = list(x = 0.05, y = 0.95)
  )

Annual electric vehicle sales (2010–2024)

Data from the IEA Global EV Outlook illustrate the rapid acceleration of EV adoption worldwide, with particularly strong growth in major markets such as China, the United States, and Europe.

Code 
df <- read.csv("data/EV.csv")
df_long <- df %>%
  pivot_longer(
    cols = -Entity, 
    names_to = "Year", 
    values_to = "EV_Sales"
  ) 
df_clean <- df_long %>%
  mutate(
    Year = str_remove(Year, "^X") %>% as.integer(),
    EV_Sales = EV_Sales %>%
      str_replace_all(",", "") %>%  
      str_trim() %>%                
      na_if("") %>%                 
      as.numeric()
  )

entities <- c("World", "China", "USA", "United Kingdom")
df_subset <- df_clean %>% filter(Entity %in% entities)

# 
ggplot(df_subset, aes(x = Year, y = EV_Sales, fill = Entity)) +
  geom_col(position = "dodge", alpha = 0.8) +
  geom_line(aes(color = Entity, group = Entity), size = 1.2) +
  geom_point(aes(color = Entity), size = 2) +
  scale_fill_brewer(palette = "Set2") +
  scale_color_brewer(palette = "Dark2") +
  labs(
    title = "Global Electric Vehicle Sales Growth (2010–2024)",
    x = "Year",
    y = "Annual EV Sales (units)",
    fill = "Region/World",
    color = "Region/World"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 14) +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

Global carbon pricing trends

The carbon price trajectory between 1990 and 2020 exhibits a pronounced peak around 1997, followed by a subsequent decline. The 1997 peak coincides with the adoption of the Kyoto Protocol and the initiation of early carbon pricing mechanisms, during which supply constraints and nascent market structures likely drove temporarily elevated prices. In the years following 2000, the global average carbon price stabilizes within the range of USD 20–40 per ton, reflecting the expansion and maturation of carbon pricing instruments—including carbon taxes and emissions trading systems—across multiple jurisdictions. This stabilization indicates a broadening of market coverage and the integration of diverse mechanisms, though prices remain below levels typically considered necessary to achieve long-term climate mitigation targets.

Code 
df <- read.csv("data/Carbonprice.csv")
df_long <- df %>%
  pivot_longer(
    cols = starts_with("X"), 
    names_to = "Year",
    values_to = "Value"
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    Year = as.integer(str_remove(Year, "^X")), 
    HasPolicy = ifelse(is.na(Value) | Value == "", 0, 1)
  )
Code 
df_world_price <- df_long %>%
  group_by(Year) %>%
  summarise(
    mean_price = mean(Value, na.rm = TRUE),
    median_price = median(Value, na.rm = TRUE)
  )

ggplot(df_world_price, aes(x = Year)) +
  geom_line(aes(y = mean_price, color = "Mean"), size = 1.2, na.rm = TRUE) +
  geom_line(aes(y = median_price, color = "Median"), size = 1.2, linetype = "dashed", na.rm = TRUE) +
  labs(title = "Global carbon pricing trends",
       x = "Year", y = "Price ($/tCO₂e)", color = "Statistic") +
  theme_minimal()

Global investment in power generation technologies (2015–2025)

Investments in renewables have grown steadily, reflecting declining technology costs and supportive policy frameworks, whereas spending on coal (unabated) continues its long-term decline due to climate concerns and competitiveness issues. Nuclear power shows moderate but consistent growth, suggesting its role as a low-carbon complement to renewables. By contrast, oil and natural gas (unabated) investments exhibit volatility, influenced by global fuel price dynamics and shifting demand expectations. The comparative evolution of these categories underscores the structural shift in global power systems, where renewables are emerging as the dominant investment destination while unabated fossil-based power steadily loses ground.

Code 
df <- read.csv("data/investment.csv")
df_long <- df %>%
  pivot_longer(
    cols = -1, 
    names_to = "Year", 
    values_to = "Investment"
  ) %>%
  rename(Category = 1) %>%
  mutate(
    Year = as.numeric(str_remove(Year, "^X")),  
    Investment = as.numeric(Investment)
  )%>%
  mutate(Type = case_when(
    Category %in% c("Fossil fuels", "Clean Fuels") ~ "Fuel",
    Category %in% c("Renewables", "Nuclear", "Coal (unabated)", "Oil and natural gas (unabated)") ~ "Power"
  ))
df_power <- df_long %>% filter(Type == "Power")
p_power <- ggplot(df_power, aes(x = Year, y = Investment, fill = Category)) +
  geom_area(alpha = 0.7) +
  labs(title = "Global Energy Investment: Power Generation Sources",
       x = "Year", y = "Investment (billion USD)") +
  theme_minimal()

p_power_interactive <- ggplotly(p_power)
